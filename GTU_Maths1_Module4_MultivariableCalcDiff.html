TOPIC 1

Limits and Continuity of Functions of Two Variables

1.1 Functions of Two Variables — Introduction ⚪ Foundation

📖 KEY DEFINITIONS

A function of two variables \(f(x,y)\) assigns a real number to each point \((x,y)\) in its domain (a region in the \(xy\)-plane).

Examples: \(f(x,y)=x^2+y^2\), \(f(x,y)=\frac{xy}{x^2+y^2}\), \(f(x,y)=e^{x+y}\).

Unlike single-variable functions, approaching a point \((a,b)\) can happen along infinitely many paths — this makes limits far more complex.

1.2 Limits of Two-Variable Functions 🔥 Most Important

📖 DEFINITION OF LIMIT

\(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)}f(x,y) = L\) means: as \((x,y)\) approaches \((a,b)\) along any path, \(f(x,y)\) approaches \(L\).

The limit exists only if the same value \(L\) is obtained regardless of direction of approach.

⭐ DIRECT SUBSTITUTION

If \(f\) is a polynomial or the point is not a problem point (no \(\frac{0}{0}\) or undefined), just substitute \((a,b)\) directly.

Example: \(\lim_{(x,y)\to(1,2)}(x^2+y) = 1+2 = 3\).

1.3 Proving a Limit Does NOT Exist — Path Method 🔥 Most Important

🔧 THE TWO-PATH TEST (Key technique for "limit does not exist" proofs)

To show \(\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}f(x,y)\) does NOT exist:

  1. Choose Path 1 (e.g., \(y=0\), i.e., along \(x\)-axis) → find limit \(L_1\)
  2. Choose Path 2 (e.g., \(y=x\) or \(y=mx\) or \(y=x^2\)) → find limit \(L_2\)
  3. If \(L_1 \neq L_2\) → the limit does not exist

Important: If all paths give the same limit, it does NOT yet prove existence — must use \(\epsilon\)-\(\delta\) or polar coordinates to confirm.

💡 INTUITION In single variable, there are only two paths to a point (from left and right). In two variables, you can approach from infinitely many directions. If even two paths give different answers — the limit is undefined.
💡 SMART PATH CHOICES FOR (0,0)
PathSubstitutionGood for detecting...
Along x-axis\(y=0\)First check — simple
Along y-axis\(x=0\)Second check
Along \(y=x\)Replace \(y\) with \(x\)Diagonal approach
Along \(y=mx\)Replace \(y\) with \(mx\)Parametric family — if limit depends on \(m\), DNE

1.4 Continuity of Two-Variable Functions ⭐ High Priority

📖 CONTINUITY DEFINITION

\(f(x,y)\) is continuous at \((a,b)\) if:

  1. \(f(a,b)\) is defined
  2. \(\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)}f(x,y)\) exists
  3. \(\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)}f(x,y) = f(a,b)\)

All three conditions must hold.

🔧 METHOD: Checking Continuity at a Point (Typically at Origin)
  1. Find \(f(0,0)\) from definition (usually given as a piecewise value)
  2. Compute \(\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}f(x,y)\) using path method
  3. If limit \(\neq f(0,0)\) OR limit doesn't exist → NOT continuous
  4. If limit \(= f(0,0)\) → continuous

1.5 PYQs — Limits and Continuity 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PYQs — LIMITS AND CONTINUITY

Q: Determine whether \(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{2x^2y}{x^3+y^3}\) exists and find it if it exists.
Winter 2023 3 Marks 🔥 Classic path test
💡 Use the two-path method — try paths that give different limits.
PATH 1 — Along \(y=0\): \[f(x,0) = \frac{2x^2\cdot 0}{x^3+0} = 0 \xrightarrow{x\to 0} 0\]
PATH 2 — Along \(y=x\): \[f(x,x) = \frac{2x^2\cdot x}{x^3+x^3} = \frac{2x^3}{2x^3} = 1 \xrightarrow{x\to 0} 1\]
CONCLUSION: Along Path 1, limit \(= 0\). Along Path 2, limit \(= 1\). Since \(0 \neq 1\), the limit depends on the path taken.
∴ The limit does NOT EXIST.
Q: Does \(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{2x^2y}{x^4+2y^2}\) exist? Find it if it does.
Winter 2022 3 Marks
💡 Try paths \(y=0\), \(y=x\), and crucially \(y=x^2\) (which matches the \(x^4\) in denominator).
PATH 1 — \(y=0\): \(f(x,0)=\frac{0}{x^4}=0\to 0\)
PATH 2 — \(y=x^2\): \[f(x,x^2) = \frac{2x^2\cdot x^2}{x^4+2x^4} = \frac{2x^4}{3x^4} = \frac{2}{3}\to\frac{2}{3}\]
CONCLUSION: Path 1 gives \(0\), Path 2 gives \(\frac{2}{3}\). Since \(0\neq\frac{2}{3}\):
∴ The limit does NOT EXIST.
The \(y=x^2\) path is the "killer" here because it eliminates the \(x^4\) from the denominator, revealing the true behavior.
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{\sqrt{x}-\sqrt{y}}{\sqrt{x}+\sqrt{y}}\)
Winter 2025 3 Marks
💡 Two-path test — try \(y=0\) and \(x=0\).
PATH 1 — Along \(y=0\) (so \(x\to 0^+\)): \[\frac{\sqrt{x}-0}{\sqrt{x}+0} = \frac{\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{x}} = 1 \to 1\]
PATH 2 — Along \(x=0\) (so \(y\to 0^+\)): \[\frac{0-\sqrt{y}}{0+\sqrt{y}} = \frac{-\sqrt{y}}{\sqrt{y}} = -1 \to -1\]
CONCLUSION: \(1\neq -1\).
∴ The limit does NOT EXIST.
Q: Show that \(\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{x^4-y^2}{x^4+y^2}\) does not exist.
Summer 2023 4 Marks
PATH 1 — \(y=0\): \[\frac{x^4-0}{x^4+0}=1\to 1\]
PATH 2 — \(x=0\): \[\frac{0-y^2}{0+y^2}=-1\to -1\]
∴ Limits differ (\(1\neq -1\)) → Limit does NOT EXIST. ✓
Q: If \(f(x,y)=\dfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2}\) for \((x,y)\neq(0,0)\) and \(f(0,0)=0\), does the limit exist at origin? Is \(f\) continuous at \((0,0)\)?
Summer 2025 3 Marks ⭐ Also Winter 2022
💡 Show limit DNE using two paths → function is NOT continuous at origin.
PATH 1 — \(y=0\): \[f(x,0)=\frac{x\cdot 0}{x^2+0}=0\to 0\]
PATH 2 — \(y=x\): \[f(x,x)=\frac{x\cdot x}{x^2+x^2}=\frac{x^2}{2x^2}=\frac{1}{2}\to\frac{1}{2}\]
CONCLUSION: \(0\neq\frac{1}{2}\) → limit does NOT exist. Since the limit doesn't exist, \(f\) is NOT continuous at \((0,0)\).
∴ Limit does NOT EXIST → \(f\) is NOT continuous at \((0,0)\).
Q: Discuss continuity of \(f(x,y)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{2x^2y}{x^4+2y^2} & (x,y)\neq(0,0)\\0 & (x,y)=(0,0)\end{cases}\) at the origin.
Winter 2025 (OR) 3 Marks
💡 We already showed this limit DNE above (using \(y=x^2\) path). Since limit DNE, function is discontinuous.
PATH \(y=x^2\): \[f(x,x^2)=\frac{2x^2\cdot x^2}{x^4+2x^4}=\frac{2x^4}{3x^4}=\frac{2}{3}\neq 0=f(0,0)\]
∴ Limit \(\neq f(0,0)\) → \(f\) is NOT continuous at \((0,0)\).

TOPIC 2

Partial Derivatives and Total Derivative

2.1 Partial Derivatives — Definition and Computation 🔥 Most Important

📖 PARTIAL DERIVATIVES

Partial derivative w.r.t. \(x\): Treat \(y\) as constant, differentiate w.r.t. \(x\): \[f_x = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h,y)-f(x,y)}{h}\]

Partial derivative w.r.t. \(y\): Treat \(x\) as constant, differentiate w.r.t. \(y\): \[f_y = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x,y+h)-f(x,y)}{h}\]

💡 INTUITION A partial derivative measures the rate of change of \(f\) in one direction while keeping everything else frozen. It's like asking "how does temperature change if I walk east, keeping my north–south position fixed?"
⭐ SECOND-ORDER PARTIAL DERIVATIVES
NotationMeaning
\(f_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\)Differentiate w.r.t. \(x\) twice
\(f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}\)Differentiate w.r.t. \(y\) twice
\(f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\partial x}\)First \(\partial x\), then \(\partial y\)
\(f_{yx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\partial y}\)First \(\partial y\), then \(\partial x\)

Clairaut's Theorem: If \(f_{xy}\) and \(f_{yx}\) are both continuous, then \(f_{xy} = f_{yx}\).

2.2 Chain Rule for Multivariable Functions 🔥 Most Important

⭐ CHAIN RULE FORMULAS

Case 1: If \(w = f(x,y)\), \(x=x(t)\), \(y=y(t)\): \[\frac{dw}{dt} = \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}\]

Case 2: If \(w=f(x,y)\), \(x=x(s,t)\), \(y=y(s,t)\): \[\frac{\partial w}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}\] \[\frac{\partial w}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\]

Case 3 (3 variables): If \(w=f(x,y,z)\), \(x,y,z\) all functions of \(t\): \[\frac{dw}{dt} = \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\frac{dz}{dt}\]

🔧 STEP-BY-STEP FOR CHAIN RULE
  1. Find all partial derivatives of \(w\) (i.e., \(\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\), \(\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\), etc.)
  2. Find derivatives of \(x, y, z\) with respect to the parameter
  3. Multiply corresponding pairs and sum them up

2.3 Euler's Theorem for Homogeneous Functions ⭐ High Priority

📖 HOMOGENEOUS FUNCTION

\(f(x,y)\) is homogeneous of degree \(n\) if \(f(tx,ty) = t^n f(x,y)\) for all \(t\).

Example: \(f(x,y)=x^2+xy+y^2\) is homogeneous of degree 2 (every term has total degree 2).

⭐ EULER'S THEOREM

If \(f(x,y)\) is homogeneous of degree \(n\), then: \[\boxed{x\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + y\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = n\cdot f(x,y)}\]

Corollary (Second order): \[x^2 f_{xx} + 2xy f_{xy} + y^2 f_{yy} = n(n-1)f\]

💡 INTUITION Euler's theorem is a shortcut: instead of finding \(x\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}+y\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\) by differentiation, just identify the degree \(n\) and the answer is \(nf\). Very useful for verification problems.

2.4 PYQs — Partial Derivatives and Chain Rule 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PYQs — PARTIAL DERIVATIVES

Q: If \(w=x^2+y^2\), \(x=r-s\), \(y=r+s\), using chain rule prove that \(\dfrac{\partial w}{\partial s} = 4s\).
Winter 2023 3 Marks 🔥 Classic chain rule
💡 Apply chain rule Case 2. Find \(\partial w/\partial x\), \(\partial w/\partial y\), then \(\partial x/\partial s\), \(\partial y/\partial s\).
STEP 1 — Partial derivatives of \(w\): \[\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}=2x,\quad\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}=2y\]
STEP 2 — Partial derivatives of \(x,y\) w.r.t. \(s\): \[\frac{\partial x}{\partial s}=-1,\quad\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}=1\]
STEP 3 — Chain rule: \[\frac{\partial w}{\partial s}=\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\cdot\frac{\partial x}{\partial s}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\cdot\frac{\partial y}{\partial s} = 2x(-1)+2y(1) = -2x+2y\]
STEP 4 — Substitute \(x=r-s\), \(y=r+s\): \[-2(r-s)+2(r+s) = -2r+2s+2r+2s = 4s\]
∴ \(\dfrac{\partial w}{\partial s} = 4s\) ✓ Proved.
Q: If \(z=\tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right)\), \(x=u\cos v\), \(y=u\sin v\), find \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial u}\) and \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial v}\) using chain rule.
Winter 2022 4 Marks
💡 \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}\), \(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=\frac{-x}{x^2+y^2}\). Note \(x^2+y^2=u^2\).
PARTIAL DERIVATIVES: \[\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=\frac{1}{1+(x/y)^2}\cdot\frac{1}{y}=\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}=\frac{y}{u^2}\] \[\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=\frac{1}{1+(x/y)^2}\cdot\frac{-x}{y^2}=\frac{-x}{x^2+y^2}=\frac{-x}{u^2}\]
\(\partial z/\partial u\): \[\frac{\partial z}{\partial u}=\frac{y}{u^2}\cdot\cos v+\frac{-x}{u^2}\cdot\sin v=\frac{u\sin v\cos v-u\cos v\sin v}{u^2}=\frac{0}{u^2}=0\]
\(\partial z/\partial v\): \[\frac{\partial z}{\partial v}=\frac{y}{u^2}\cdot(-u\sin v)+\frac{-x}{u^2}\cdot(u\cos v)=\frac{-y\sin v-x\cos v}{u}\] \[=\frac{-u\sin^2 v-u\cos^2 v}{u}=-(sin^2v+\cos^2v)=-1\]
∴ \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial u}=0\) and \(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial v}=-1\)
Q: If \(u=\log(x^2+y^2)\), show that \(\dfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\dfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=0\).
Summer 2023 4 Marks 🔥 Laplace equation — repeated
💡 This asks to verify the Laplace equation \(\nabla^2 u=0\) (harmonic function). Find second-order partials.
FIRST PARTIALS: \[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=\frac{2x}{x^2+y^2},\quad\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=\frac{2y}{x^2+y^2}\]
SECOND PARTIAL W.R.T. \(x\) (quotient rule): \[\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}=\frac{2(x^2+y^2)-2x\cdot 2x}{(x^2+y^2)^2}=\frac{2y^2-2x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\]
SECOND PARTIAL W.R.T. \(y\): \[\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=\frac{2(x^2+y^2)-2y\cdot 2y}{(x^2+y^2)^2}=\frac{2x^2-2y^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\]
SUM: \[\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=\frac{2y^2-2x^2+2x^2-2y^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}=\frac{0}{(x^2+y^2)^2}=0\]
∴ \(\dfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\dfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=0\) ✓ Proved. (\(u\) is a harmonic function.)
Q: If \(z=x^2y+3xy^4\), where \(x=\sin 2t\) and \(y=\cos t\), find \(\dfrac{dz}{dt}\) when \(t=0\).
Summer 2025 4 Marks
💡 Chain rule with single parameter \(t\).
PARTIAL DERIVATIVES: \[\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=2xy+3y^4,\quad\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=x^2+12xy^3\]
DERIVATIVES W.R.T. \(t\): \[\frac{dx}{dt}=2\cos 2t,\quad\frac{dy}{dt}=-\sin t\]
CHAIN RULE: \[\frac{dz}{dt}=(2xy+3y^4)(2\cos 2t)+(x^2+12xy^3)(-\sin t)\]
AT \(t=0\): \(x=\sin 0=0\), \(y=\cos 0=1\): \[\frac{dz}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0}=(0+3)(2\cos 0)+(0+0)(-\sin 0)=3\cdot 2\cdot 1=6\]
∴ \(\dfrac{dz}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0}=6\)

TOPIC 3

Gradient and Directional Derivatives

3.1 Gradient Vector — Definition and Properties 🔥 Most Important

📖 GRADIENT

For \(f(x,y,z)\), the gradient is the vector: \[\nabla f = \text{grad}\,f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\,\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\,\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\,\mathbf{k}\]

For \(f(x,y)\): \(\nabla f = f_x\,\mathbf{i} + f_y\,\mathbf{j}\)

⭐ KEY PROPERTIES OF GRADIENT
  • \(\nabla f\) points in the direction of maximum rate of increase of \(f\)
  • \(-\nabla f\) points in the direction of maximum decrease
  • \(|\nabla f|\) = maximum rate of change (steepest ascent rate)
  • \(\nabla f\) is perpendicular to the level curve \(f=c\)
  • \(\nabla f\) at a point on surface \(f(x,y,z)=c\) is the normal vector to the surface

3.2 Directional Derivative 🔥 Most Important

📖 DIRECTIONAL DERIVATIVE

The directional derivative of \(f\) at point \(P\) in the direction of unit vector \(\mathbf{u}=\langle u_1,u_2,u_3\rangle\) is: \[D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = f_x u_1 + f_y u_2 + f_z u_3\]

It measures the rate of change of \(f\) in the direction \(\mathbf{u}\).

🔧 COMPLETE STEP-BY-STEP METHOD
  1. Find the gradient: \(\nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y, f_z\rangle\)
  2. Evaluate \(\nabla f\) at the given point
  3. Find the unit vector \(\mathbf{u}\) in the given direction \(\mathbf{v}\): \(\mathbf{u}=\dfrac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}\)
  4. Compute \(D_\mathbf{u}f = \nabla f\cdot\mathbf{u}\)
⭐ MAGNITUDE OF A VECTOR (Needed to find unit vector) \[|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2}\] \[\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|} = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{v}|}\langle v_1, v_2, v_3\rangle\]
⚠️ MOST COMMON MISTAKE

Students forget to convert the direction vector to a UNIT VECTOR before computing the dot product. The given vector \(\mathbf{v}\) must be normalised: \(\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{v}/|\mathbf{v}|\).

❌ WRONG: \(D_\mathbf{u}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{v}\) (using \(\mathbf{v}\) directly)

✅ RIGHT: \(D_\mathbf{u}f = \nabla f \cdot \dfrac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}\)

3.3 PYQs — Gradient and Directional Derivatives 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PYQs — DIRECTIONAL DERIVATIVES

Q: Find the directional derivative of \(f(x,y,z)=2x^2+3y^2+z^2\) at \((2,1,3)\) in the direction of \(\mathbf{i}-2\mathbf{k}\).
Winter 2023 4 Marks 🔥 Classic 3D
💡 Four steps: (1) find gradient, (2) evaluate at point, (3) find unit vector, (4) dot product.
STEP 1 — Gradient: \[\nabla f = \langle 4x,\; 6y,\; 2z\rangle\]
STEP 2 — At \((2,1,3)\): \[\nabla f\big|_{(2,1,3)} = \langle 8,\; 6,\; 6\rangle\]
STEP 3 — Unit vector in direction \(\mathbf{v}=\langle 1,0,-2\rangle\): \[|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt{1+0+4}=\sqrt{5},\quad\mathbf{u}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\langle 1,0,-2\rangle\]
STEP 4 — Directional derivative: \[D_\mathbf{u}f = \langle 8,6,6\rangle\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\langle 1,0,-2\rangle = \frac{8(1)+6(0)+6(-2)}{\sqrt{5}} = \frac{8-12}{\sqrt{5}} = \frac{-4}{\sqrt{5}}\]
∴ Directional derivative \(= \dfrac{-4}{\sqrt{5}} = \dfrac{-4\sqrt{5}}{5}\)
The negative sign means \(f\) is decreasing in this direction.
Q: Find the directional derivative of \(f(x,y)=x^2\sin 2y\) at \(\left(1,\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\) in the direction of \(\mathbf{v}=3\hat{i}-4\hat{j}\).
Winter 2022 3 Marks ⭐ 2D version
GRADIENT: \[f_x=2x\sin 2y,\quad f_y=2x^2\cos 2y\] At \((1,\pi/2)\): \(f_x=2\sin\pi=0\), \(f_y=2\cos\pi=-2\). \[\nabla f\big|_{(1,\pi/2)}=\langle 0,-2\rangle\]
UNIT VECTOR: \(|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt{9+16}=5\), \(\mathbf{u}=\frac{1}{5}\langle 3,-4\rangle\).
DOT PRODUCT: \[D_\mathbf{u}f=\langle 0,-2\rangle\cdot\frac{1}{5}\langle 3,-4\rangle=\frac{0+8}{5}=\frac{8}{5}\]
∴ Directional derivative \(=\dfrac{8}{5}\)
Q: Find the directional derivative of \(f(x,y,z)=x^2yz+4xz^2\) at \((1,-2,-1)\) in direction \(2\hat{i}-\hat{j}-2\hat{k}\).
Summer 2024 3 Marks
GRADIENT: \[f_x=2xyz+4z^2,\quad f_y=x^2z,\quad f_z=x^2y+8xz\] At \((1,-2,-1)\): \[f_x=2(1)(-2)(-1)+4(1)=4+4=8\] \[f_y=(1)(-1)=-1\] \[f_z=(1)(-2)+8(1)(-1)=-2-8=-10\] \[\nabla f=\langle 8,-1,-10\rangle\]
UNIT VECTOR: \(|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt{4+1+4}=3\), \(\mathbf{u}=\frac{1}{3}\langle 2,-1,-2\rangle\).
DOT PRODUCT: \[D_\mathbf{u}f=\frac{1}{3}(16+1+20)=\frac{37}{3}\]
∴ Directional derivative \(=\dfrac{37}{3}\)
Q: Find the directional derivative of \(f(x,y)=x^2e^{xy}\) at \((1,0)\) in direction \(\bar{u}=\hat{i}-\hat{j}\).
Winter 2025 (OR) 4 Marks
GRADIENT: \[f_x=2xe^{xy}+x^2ye^{xy}=e^{xy}(2x+x^2y)\] \[f_y=x^3e^{xy}\] At \((1,0)\): \(f_x=e^0(2+0)=2\), \(f_y=1\cdot e^0=1\). \[\nabla f=\langle 2,1\rangle\]
UNIT VECTOR: \(\mathbf{u}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\langle 1,-1\rangle\).
DOT PRODUCT: \[D_\mathbf{u}f=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(2-1)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\]
∴ Directional derivative \(=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\)

TOPIC 4

Tangent Plane and Normal Line to a Surface

4.1 Equation of Tangent Plane 🔥 Most Important

⭐ TANGENT PLANE FORMULAS

Case 1 — Surface as \(F(x,y,z)=0\) at point \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\): \[F_x(x_0,y_0,z_0)(x-x_0)+F_y(x_0,y_0,z_0)(y-y_0)+F_z(x_0,y_0,z_0)(z-z_0)=0\]

Case 2 — Surface as \(z=f(x,y)\) at point \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\): \[z-z_0 = f_x(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0)+f_y(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0)\]

💡 INTUITION The tangent plane at a point on a surface touches the surface at that point and is flat (locally). The normal to the surface is the gradient vector \(\nabla F\). Tangent plane = plane perpendicular to this normal, passing through the point.

4.2 Equation of Normal Line 🔥 Most Important

⭐ NORMAL LINE FORMULA

The normal line to \(F(x,y,z)=0\) at \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\) passes through \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\) in the direction \(\nabla F = \langle F_x, F_y, F_z\rangle\):

\[\frac{x-x_0}{F_x} = \frac{y-y_0}{F_y} = \frac{z-z_0}{F_z}\]

where all partial derivatives are evaluated at \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\).

🔧 COMPLETE METHOD FOR TANGENT PLANE AND NORMAL LINE
  1. Write surface as \(F(x,y,z)=0\) (move everything to one side)
  2. Compute \(F_x, F_y, F_z\)
  3. Evaluate at the given point \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\)
  4. Write tangent plane equation using the formula
  5. Write normal line as symmetric equations

TOPIC 5

Extreme Values of Functions of Two Variables

5.1 Critical Points — Definition 🔥 Most Important

📖 CRITICAL POINT

A point \((a,b)\) is a critical point of \(f(x,y)\) if \(f_x(a,b)=0\) and \(f_y(a,b)=0\).

Critical points are candidates for local maxima, local minima, or saddle points.

5.2 Second Derivative Test for Two Variables 🔥 Most Important

⭐ SECOND DERIVATIVE TEST (2D) — MUST MEMORIZE

At a critical point \((a,b)\), compute the discriminant (Hessian determinant): \[D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2\]

ConditionConclusion
\(D>0\) and \(f_{xx}>0\)Local MINIMUM
\(D>0\) and \(f_{xx}<0\)Local MAXIMUM
\(D<0\)SADDLE POINT (neither max nor min)
\(D=0\)INCONCLUSIVE — test fails, use other methods
🔧 COMPLETE STEP-BY-STEP METHOD
  1. Find \(f_x\) and \(f_y\)
  2. Solve \(f_x=0\) AND \(f_y=0\) simultaneously → get critical points
  3. Find \(f_{xx},\; f_{yy},\; f_{xy}\)
  4. Compute \(D=f_{xx}f_{yy}-(f_{xy})^2\) at each critical point
  5. Apply the table above to classify each critical point
  6. Find the extreme values: substitute back into \(f(x,y)\)
⚠️ COMMON MISTAKES
  • Finding \(D\) but forgetting to also check sign of \(f_{xx}\) when \(D>0\)
  • Confusing \(D=f_{xx}f_{yy}-(f_{xy})^2\) with \(f_{xx}f_{yy}-f_{xx}f_{yy}\) — the mixed partial appears squared!
  • Not solving the system \(f_x=0, f_y=0\) simultaneously — must find all critical points
  • Forgetting to compute the actual extreme VALUE \(f(a,b)\)

TOPIC 6

Method of Lagrange Multipliers

6.1 Lagrange Multipliers — Concept and Method 🔥 Most Important

📖 PROBLEM TYPE

Find the maximum or minimum of \(f(x,y,z)\) subject to a constraint \(g(x,y,z)=0\).

Key Idea: At a constrained extremum, the gradient of \(f\) must be parallel to the gradient of \(g\). That is, \(\nabla f = \lambda\nabla g\) for some scalar \(\lambda\) (the Lagrange multiplier).

🔧 COMPLETE LAGRANGE MULTIPLIERS METHOD
  1. Write the constraint as \(g(x,y,z)=0\)
  2. Set up the system: \[\nabla f = \lambda\nabla g\quad\text{i.e.,}\quad f_x=\lambda g_x,\;\; f_y=\lambda g_y,\;\; f_z=\lambda g_z\]
  3. This gives 3 equations + 1 constraint = 4 equations in 4 unknowns \((x,y,z,\lambda)\)
  4. Solve the system to find candidate points
  5. Evaluate \(f\) at all candidate points
  6. Largest value = maximum, smallest value = minimum
💡 INTUITION Imagine you're on a hillside (the constraint surface) and you want to find the highest point. At the top/bottom, the hill's slope must perfectly oppose or align with the constraint surface's gradient — they must point in the same direction. This is exactly \(\nabla f = \lambda\nabla g\).
⭐ FOR 2 VARIABLES: Maximize/Minimize \(f(x,y)\) subject to \(g(x,y)=0\)

System: \(f_x = \lambda g_x\), \(f_y = \lambda g_y\), \(g(x,y)=0\).

Solve these 3 equations for \(x, y, \lambda\).

6.2 PYQs — Lagrange Multipliers 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PYQs — LAGRANGE MULTIPLIERS

Q: Find the maximum and minimum values of \(f(x,y)=3x+4y\) on the circle \(x^2+y^2=1\).
Winter 2023 7 Marks 🔥 Most Classic Lagrange
💡 f = objective, g = constraint. \(g = x^2+y^2-1=0\). Set up \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\).
STEP 1 — Gradients: \[\nabla f = \langle 3,4\rangle,\quad\nabla g = \langle 2x, 2y\rangle\]
STEP 2 — System \(\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g\): \[3=2\lambda x\Rightarrow x=\frac{3}{2\lambda}\quad\cdots(1)\] \[4=2\lambda y\Rightarrow y=\frac{4}{2\lambda}=\frac{2}{\lambda}\quad\cdots(2)\]
STEP 3 — Substitute into constraint \(x^2+y^2=1\): \[\frac{9}{4\lambda^2}+\frac{4}{\lambda^2}=1\Rightarrow\frac{9+16}{4\lambda^2}=1\Rightarrow\frac{25}{4\lambda^2}=1\Rightarrow\lambda^2=\frac{25}{4}\Rightarrow\lambda=\pm\frac{5}{2}\]
STEP 4 — Find candidate points: \(\lambda=\frac{5}{2}\): \(x=\frac{3}{5}\), \(y=\frac{4}{5}\). \(\lambda=-\frac{5}{2}\): \(x=-\frac{3}{5}\), \(y=-\frac{4}{5}\).
STEP 5 — Evaluate \(f\): \[f\!\left(\tfrac{3}{5},\tfrac{4}{5}\right)=\frac{9}{5}+\frac{16}{5}=\frac{25}{5}=5\] \[f\!\left(-\tfrac{3}{5},-\tfrac{4}{5}\right)=-5\]
Maximum \(= 5\) at \(\left(\frac{3}{5},\frac{4}{5}\right)\). Minimum \(= -5\) at \(\left(-\frac{3}{5},-\frac{4}{5}\right)\).
Q: Find the maximum and minimum values of \(f(x,y)=x^2+2y^2\) on the circle \(x^2+y^2=1\).

SYSTEM: \[\nabla f=\langle 2x,4y\rangle=\lambda\langle 2x,2y\rangle\] \(2x=2\lambda x\Rightarrow\lambda=1\) (if \(x\neq 0\)) or \(x=0\). \(4y=2\lambda y\Rightarrow\lambda=2\) (if \(y\neq 0\)) or \(y=0\).
CASE 1 — \(\lambda=1\): \(y=0\), constraint: \(x^2=1\), so \(x=\pm 1\). Points: \((1,0),(-1,0)\).
CASE 2 — \(\lambda=2\): \(x=0\), constraint: \(y^2=1\), so \(y=\pm 1\). Points: \((0,1),(0,-1)\).
EVALUATE: \(f(1,0)=1\), \(f(-1,0)=1\), \(f(0,1)=2\), \(f(0,-1)=2\).
Maximum \(= 2\) at \((0,\pm 1)\). Minimum \(= 1\) at \((\pm 1,0)\).

Winter 2024 7 Marks 🔥 Application problem
💡 Minimize surface area \(S = xy + 2xz + 2yz\) (open top: no top face) subject to volume constraint \(V=xyz=32\).

Q: Find the shortest and longest distances from point \((1,1,1)\) to the sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=1\).

💡 Minimize/maximize \(d^2 = (x-1)^2+(y-1)^2+(z-1)^2\) subject to \(x^2+y^2+z^2=1\). (Using \(d^2\) instead of \(d\) avoids square roots.)
