TOPIC 1

Double Integrals Over Rectangular Regions

1.1 Double Integral — Definition and Fubini's Theorem 🔥 Most Important

📖 DOUBLE INTEGRAL

The double integral of \(f(x,y)\) over a region \(R\) is: \[\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA\] It represents the signed volume between the surface \(z=f(x,y)\) and the \(xy\)-plane over region \(R\).

⭐ FUBINI'S THEOREM (How to evaluate a double integral)

For a rectangular region \(R: a\leq x\leq b,\; c\leq y\leq d\): \[\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA = \int_a^b\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx = \int_c^d\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy\]

Key: Integrate the inner integral first (treat outer variable as constant), then integrate the result.

💡 INTUITION Think of a double integral as slicing a 3D solid into thin slabs — integrate first in one direction (getting area of each slab), then in the other direction (adding up all slabs). The order doesn't matter for rectangular regions.

1.2 Evaluating Double Integrals — Step by Step 🔥 Most Important

🔧 STANDARD METHOD
  1. Identify limits for inner and outer integrals from the region \(R\)
  2. Evaluate inner integral (treating outer variable as constant)
  3. Evaluate outer integral on the result

Example: \(\int_0^2\int_{-1}^1 (100-6x^2y)\,dy\,dx\)

Inner: \(\int_{-1}^1(100-6x^2y)\,dy = [100y-3x^2y^2]_{-1}^1 = (100-3x^2)-(- 100-3x^2) = 200\)

Outer: \(\int_0^2 200\,dx = 400\)

1.3 PYQs — Rectangular Double Integrals 🔥 Most Important

🎯 PYQs — DOUBLE INTEGRALS (RECTANGULAR REGIONS)

Q: Calculate \(\displaystyle\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA\) for \(f(x,y)=100-6x^2y\) and \(R: 0\leq x\leq 2,\; -1\leq y\leq 1\).
Winter 2023 3 Marks
INNER INTEGRAL (w.r.t. \(y\)): \[\int_{-1}^1(100-6x^2y)\,dy = \Big[100y-3x^2y^2\Big]_{-1}^1 = (100-3x^2)-(-100-3x^2) = 200\]
OUTER INTEGRAL (w.r.t. \(x\)): \[\int_0^2 200\,dx = \Big[200x\Big]_0^2 = 400\]
∴ \(\displaystyle\iint_R (100-6x^2y)\,dA = 400\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^3\int_0^1 (x^2+3y^2)\,dy\,dx\)
Summer 2024 3 Marks
INNER (w.r.t. \(y\), 0 to 1): \[\int_0^1(x^2+3y^2)\,dy = \Big[x^2y+y^3\Big]_0^1 = x^2+1\]
OUTER (w.r.t. \(x\), 0 to 3): \[\int_0^3(x^2+1)\,dx = \Big[\frac{x^3}{3}+x\Big]_0^3 = 9+3 = 12\]
∴ Answer \(= 12\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\iint_R(x+y)^2\,dA\) where \(R=[0,1]\times[0,2]\).
Winter 2025 3 Marks
SET UP: \[\int_0^1\int_0^2(x+y)^2\,dy\,dx\]
INNER (w.r.t. \(y\)): \[\int_0^2(x+y)^2\,dy = \left[\frac{(x+y)^3}{3}\right]_0^2 = \frac{(x+2)^3-(x)^3}{3}\]
OUTER (w.r.t. \(x\), 0 to 1): \[\int_0^1\frac{(x+2)^3-x^3}{3}\,dx = \frac{1}{3}\left[\frac{(x+2)^4}{4}-\frac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{12}\left[(3^4-2^4)-(1^4-0)\right]=\frac{1}{12}(81-16-1)=\frac{64}{12}=\frac{16}{3}\]
∴ \(\displaystyle\iint_R(x+y)^2\,dA = \dfrac{16}{3}\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\iint_R(3x+4y^2)\,dA\) where \(R\) is the upper half-plane bounded by \(x^2+y^2=1\) and \(x^2+y^2=4\).
Summer 2025 4 Marks ⭐ Polar needed
💡 Region is annular (between two semicircles) → use polar coordinates. \(1\leq r\leq 2\), \(0\leq\theta\leq\pi\).
POLAR TRANSFORM: \(x=r\cos\theta\), \(y=r\sin\theta\), \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\): \[\int_0^\pi\int_1^2(3r\cos\theta+4r^2\sin^2\theta)\,r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_0^\pi\int_1^2(3r^2\cos\theta+4r^3\sin^2\theta)\,dr\,d\theta\]
INNER (w.r.t. \(r\)): \[\int_1^2(3r^2\cos\theta+4r^3\sin^2\theta)\,dr = \Big[r^3\cos\theta+r^4\sin^2\theta\Big]_1^2 = 7\cos\theta+15\sin^2\theta\]
OUTER (w.r.t. \(\theta\)): \[\int_0^\pi(7\cos\theta+15\sin^2\theta)\,d\theta = \Big[7\sin\theta\Big]_0^\pi+15\int_0^\pi\sin^2\theta\,d\theta\] \[= 0+15\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{15\pi}{2}\]
∴ \(\displaystyle\iint_R(3x+4y^2)\,dA = \dfrac{15\pi}{2}\)
TOPIC 2

Double Integrals Over General (Non-Rectangular) Regions

2.1 Non-Rectangular Regions — Setup 🔥 Most Important

⭐ TWO TYPES OF REGIONS

Type I (x-simple): \(a\leq x\leq b\), \(g_1(x)\leq y\leq g_2(x)\) \[\iint_R f\,dA = \int_a^b\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx\] (Inner integral: \(y\) from curve to curve, \(x\) is constant)

Type II (y-simple): \(c\leq y\leq d\), \(h_1(y)\leq x\leq h_2(y)\) \[\iint_R f\,dA = \int_c^d\int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy\]

🔧 HOW TO SET UP LIMITS FROM A REGION
  1. Sketch the region (draw all boundary curves)
  2. Choose order of integration (dx dy or dy dx)
  3. Outer limits: constant values (numbers)
  4. Inner limits: express one variable's bounds in terms of the other

Example: Region bounded by \(y=x\) and \(y=x^2\):

Intersection: \(x=x^2\Rightarrow x=0,1\). For \(0\leq x\leq 1\): \(x^2\leq y\leq x\).

Setup: \(\int_0^1\int_{x^2}^x f(x,y)\,dy\,dx\)

2.2 Sketching the Region of Integration 🔥 Most Important

📖 HOW TO SKETCH FROM GIVEN LIMITS

Given \(\displaystyle\int_a^b\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f\,dy\,dx\):

  1. The outer limits \(a\leq x\leq b\) give vertical bounds
  2. The inner limits \(g_1(x)\leq y\leq g_2(x)\) give the upper and lower curves
  3. Sketch these curves and shade the enclosed region
⚠️ COMMON MISTAKES IN SETTING UP LIMITS
  • Mixing up which is the outer variable and which is inner — outer limits must be CONSTANTS
  • Getting upper and lower curves reversed — always check: which curve is on top in the region?
  • Forgetting to find intersection points of boundary curves
  • For Type II integrals: express \(x\) as a function of \(y\) (solve for \(x\))

2.3 PYQs — General Region Double Integrals 🔥 Most Important

🎯 PYQs — NON-RECTANGULAR REGIONS

Q: Calculate \(\displaystyle\iint_R \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dA\) where \(R\) is the triangle bounded by the \(x\)-axis, \(y=x\) and \(x=1\).
Winter 2023 7 Marks 🔥 Classic — change of order needed
💡 The region is: \(0\leq x\leq 1\), \(0\leq y\leq x\). As given, \(\int_0^1\int_0^x\frac{\sin x}{x}\,dy\,dx\) — \(\frac{\sin x}{x}\) does NOT depend on \(y\), so inner integral is simple!
INNER INTEGRAL (w.r.t. \(y\), 0 to \(x\)): \[\int_0^x\frac{\sin x}{x}\,dy = \frac{\sin x}{x}\cdot[y]_0^x = \frac{\sin x}{x}\cdot x = \sin x\]
OUTER INTEGRAL (w.r.t. \(x\), 0 to 1): \[\int_0^1\sin x\,dx = [-\cos x]_0^1 = -\cos 1+\cos 0 = 1-\cos 1\]
∴ \(\displaystyle\iint_R\frac{\sin x}{x}\,dA = 1-\cos 1 \approx 0.4597\)
Q: Sketch region for \(\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_{x^2}^{2x}(4x+2)\,dy\,dx\) and write equivalent integral with reversed order.
Winter 2023 4 Marks ⭐ Sketch + reverse order
💡 Original: \(0\leq x\leq 2\), \(x^2\leq y\leq 2x\). Region bounded by parabola \(y=x^2\) (bottom) and line \(y=2x\) (top). Intersect: \(x=0,2\).
EVALUATE ORIGINAL: \[\int_0^2(4x+2)[y]_{x^2}^{2x}dx = \int_0^2(4x+2)(2x-x^2)\,dx = \int_0^2(8x^2-4x^3+4x-2x^2)\,dx\] \[= \int_0^2(6x^2-4x^3+4x)\,dx = [2x^3-x^4+2x^2]_0^2 = 16-16+8 = 8\]
REVERSE ORDER — describe region in terms of \(y\) first: From \(y=x^2\): \(x=\sqrt{y}\); from \(y=2x\): \(x=y/2\). For \(0\leq y\leq 4\): \(y/2\leq x\leq\sqrt{y}\). \[\int_0^4\int_{y/2}^{\sqrt{y}}(4x+2)\,dx\,dy\]
Reversed integral: \(\displaystyle\int_0^4\int_{y/2}^{\sqrt{y}}(4x+2)\,dx\,dy\)
Q: Find area of region \(R\) bounded by \(y=x\) and \(y=x^2\) in the first quadrant.
Winter 2023 4 Marks 🔥 Standard area
INTERSECTION: \(y=x\) and \(y=x^2\): \(x=x^2\Rightarrow x=0,1\).
For \(0\leq x\leq 1\): \(x^2\leq y\leq x\). Area: \[\text{Area}=\iint_R dA=\int_0^1\int_{x^2}^x dy\,dx=\int_0^1(x-x^2)\,dx=\left[\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1=\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{3}=\frac{1}{6}\]
∴ Area \(= \dfrac{1}{6}\) square units.
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_{\ln 1}^{\ln 8}\int_0^{\ln y} e^{x+y}\,dx\,dy\)
Winter 2023 (OR) 3 Marks
INNER (w.r.t. \(x\), 0 to \(\ln y\)): \[\int_0^{\ln y}e^{x+y}\,dx = e^y[e^x]_0^{\ln y} = e^y(y-1)\]
OUTER (w.r.t. \(y\), 1 to 8; note \(\ln 1=0,\ln 8=\ln 8\)): \[\int_1^8 e^y(y-1)\,dy\] Integration by parts: \(u=y-1\), \(dv=e^y dy\Rightarrow v=e^y\): \[= [e^y(y-1)]_1^8 - \int_1^8 e^y\,dy = (7e^8-0)-[e^y]_1^8 = 7e^8-(e^8-e) = 6e^8+e\]
∴ Answer \(= 6e^8+e\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\iint_R y(x-y^2)\,dy\,dx\) where \(R\) is bounded by \(y=\sqrt{x}\) and \(y=x^3\).
Winter 2022 4 Marks
INTERSECTION: \(\sqrt{x}=x^3\Rightarrow x^{1/2}=x^3\Rightarrow x=0,1\). For \(0\leq x\leq 1\): \(x^3\leq y\leq\sqrt{x}\) (since \(\sqrt{x}\geq x^3\) on \([0,1]\)).
INTEGRAL: \[\int_0^1\int_{x^3}^{\sqrt{x}}y(x-y^2)\,dy\,dx = \int_0^1\int_{x^3}^{\sqrt{x}}(xy-y^3)\,dy\,dx\]
INNER: \[\int_{x^3}^{\sqrt{x}}(xy-y^3)\,dy=\left[\frac{xy^2}{2}-\frac{y^4}{4}\right]_{x^3}^{\sqrt{x}}=\left(\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{x^2}{4}\right)-\left(\frac{x^7}{2}-\frac{x^{12}}{4}\right)=\frac{x^2}{4}-\frac{x^7}{2}+\frac{x^{12}}{4}\]
OUTER: \[\int_0^1\left(\frac{x^2}{4}-\frac{x^7}{2}+\frac{x^{12}}{4}\right)dx=\frac{1}{12}-\frac{1}{16}+\frac{1}{52}=\frac{52-39+12}{624}\cdot... \] Computing: \(\frac{1}{12}-\frac{1}{16}+\frac{1}{52} = \frac{52-39+12}{624}=\frac{25}{624}\)
∴ \(\displaystyle\iint_R y(x-y^2)\,dA = \dfrac{25}{624}\)
TOPIC 3

Change of Order of Integration

3.1 Why Change Order? 🔥 Most Important

💡 MOTIVATION Sometimes \(\int f\,dy\) has no closed form but \(\int f\,dx\) is easy (e.g., \(\int e^{-y^2}\,dy\) has no elementary form, but after changing order we get a solvable integral). Changing the order of integration can turn an impossible integral into a simple one.

3.2 Step-by-Step Method for Changing Order 🔥 Most Important

🔧 COMPLETE METHOD — Change Order from dy dx to dx dy
  1. Read the original limits: \(x\) from \(a\) to \(b\), \(y\) from \(g_1(x)\) to \(g_2(x)\)
  2. Sketch the region using those limits — plot all boundary curves
  3. Re-describe the region with \(y\) as outer variable: find \(y\) range (constants), express \(x\) limits as functions of \(y\)
  4. Write the new integral: \(\int_c^d\int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy\)
  5. Evaluate

Key Rule: The region of integration is fixed — you're just describing it differently.

3.3 PYQs — Change of Order of Integration 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PYQs — CHANGE OF ORDER

Q: Sketch the region, change the order of integration and evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^\infty\int_x^\infty e^{-y^2}\,dy\,dx\)
Summer 2025 7 Marks 🔥 Classic — Gaussian integral
💡 Cannot integrate \(e^{-y^2}\) w.r.t. \(y\). Must change order first. The region is \(0\leq x<\infty\), \(x\leq y<\infty\) — this is the region above the line \(y=x\) in the first quadrant.
STEP 1 — Identify region: Original: outer \(x: 0\to\infty\), inner \(y: x\to\infty\). Region: \(\{(x,y): 0\leq x, x\leq y\} = \{0\leq x\leq y, 0\leq y<\infty\}\).
STEP 2 — Re-describe: Fix \(y\): \(x\) ranges from \(0\) to \(y\); \(y\) ranges from \(0\) to \(\infty\).
STEP 3 — New integral: \[\int_0^\infty\int_0^y e^{-y^2}\,dx\,dy\]
STEP 4 — Evaluate (inner w.r.t. \(x\)): \[\int_0^y e^{-y^2}\,dx = e^{-y^2}[x]_0^y = ye^{-y^2}\]
STEP 5 — Outer: \[\int_0^\infty ye^{-y^2}\,dy = \left[-\frac{e^{-y^2}}{2}\right]_0^\infty = 0+\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\]
∴ \(\displaystyle\int_0^\infty\int_x^\infty e^{-y^2}\,dy\,dx = \dfrac{1}{2}\)
Q: Change order and evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^4\int_{\sqrt{x}}^2\frac{1}{y^3+1}\,dy\,dx\)
Summer 2023 7 Marks 🔥 Repeated pattern
💡 Cannot integrate \(\frac{1}{y^3+1}\) w.r.t. \(y\) easily. Change order to integrate w.r.t. \(x\) first.
IDENTIFY REGION: Original: \(0\leq x\leq 4\), \(\sqrt{x}\leq y\leq 2\). So region is above \(y=\sqrt{x}\) (i.e. \(x\leq y^2\)), below \(y=2\), right of \(x=0\).
RE-DESCRIBE with \(y\) outer: Fix \(y\in[0,2]\): \(x\) goes from \(0\) to \(y^2\) (since \(x\leq y^2\)). \[\int_0^2\int_0^{y^2}\frac{1}{y^3+1}\,dx\,dy\]
INNER (w.r.t. \(x\)): \[\int_0^{y^2}\frac{1}{y^3+1}\,dx = \frac{y^2}{y^3+1}\]
OUTER (w.r.t. \(y\)): \[\int_0^2\frac{y^2}{y^3+1}\,dy = \frac{1}{3}\Big[\ln(y^3+1)\Big]_0^2 = \frac{1}{3}(\ln 9-\ln 1) = \frac{\ln 9}{3} = \frac{2\ln 3}{3}\]
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{2\ln 3}{3}\)
Q: Change order and evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_x^1\sin(y^2)\,dy\,dx\)
Summer 2025 / Winter 2025 7 Marks 🔥 Repeated
💡 \(\int\sin(y^2)\,dy\) has no closed form — must change order first.
REGION: \(0\leq x\leq 1\), \(x\leq y\leq 1\). This is the triangle below \(y=1\), above \(y=x\).
RE-DESCRIBE: Fix \(y\in[0,1]\): \(x\) from \(0\) to \(y\). \[\int_0^1\int_0^y\sin(y^2)\,dx\,dy\]
INNER: \[\int_0^y\sin(y^2)\,dx = y\sin(y^2)\]
OUTER: \[\int_0^1 y\sin(y^2)\,dy = \left[-\frac{\cos(y^2)}{2}\right]_0^1 = -\frac{\cos 1}{2}+\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1-\cos 1}{2}\]
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{1-\cos 1}{2}\)
Q: Change order and evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_{2x}^4\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi y^2}{4}\right)\,dy\,dx\)
Summer 2024 7 Marks
REGION: \(0\leq x\leq 2\), \(2x\leq y\leq 4\) → above line \(y=2x\), below \(y=4\).
RE-DESCRIBE: Fix \(y\in[0,4]\): \(x\) from \(0\) to \(y/2\). \[\int_0^4\int_0^{y/2}\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi y^2}{4}\right)\,dx\,dy\]
INNER: \(\displaystyle\int_0^{y/2}\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi y^2}{4}\right)dx = \frac{y}{2}\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi y^2}{4}\right)\)
OUTER: Let \(u=\frac{\pi y^2}{4}\Rightarrow du=\frac{\pi y}{2}dy\Rightarrow y\,dy=\frac{2}{\pi}du\): \[\int_0^4\frac{y}{2}\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi y^2}{4}\right)dy = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{4\pi}\sin u\,du = \frac{1}{\pi}[-\cos u]_0^{4\pi}=\frac{1}{\pi}(-1+1)=0\] Wait: at \(y=4\), \(u=\frac{\pi\cdot 16}{4}=4\pi\). So \(\frac{1}{\pi}[-\cos u]_0^{4\pi}=\frac{1}{\pi}(-\cos 4\pi+\cos 0)=\frac{1}{\pi}(-1+1)=0\).
∴ Answer \(= 0\)
TOPIC 4

Double Integrals in Polar Coordinates

4.1 Polar Coordinate Transformation 🔥 Most Important

⭐ POLAR TRANSFORMATION — MUST MEMORIZE
CartesianPolar
\(x = r\cos\theta\)\(r^2 = x^2+y^2\)
\(y = r\sin\theta\)\(\tan\theta = y/x\)
\(dA = dx\,dy\)\(dA = r\,dr\,d\theta\) ← don't forget \(r\)!

The Jacobian factor \(r\) is CRITICAL — forgetting it is the most common mistake!

\[\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA = \iint_{R'} f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta\]
💡 WHEN TO USE POLAR COORDINATES
  • Region is a circle, annulus, or sector
  • Integrand contains \(x^2+y^2\) (replace with \(r^2\))
  • Integrand contains \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) (replace with \(r\))
  • Region is bounded by \(x^2+y^2=a^2\)

4.2 Setting Up Polar Integrals 🔥 Most Important

🔧 STANDARD POLAR LIMITS FOR COMMON REGIONS
Region\(r\) limits\(\theta\) limits
Full circle \(x^2+y^2\leq a^2\)\(0\to a\)\(0\to 2\pi\)
Upper semicircle\(0\to a\)\(0\to \pi\)
First quadrant circle\(0\to a\)\(0\to \pi/2\)
Annulus \(a^2\leq x^2+y^2\leq b^2\)\(a\to b\)\(0\to 2\pi\)
Upper half annulus\(a\to b\)\(0\to \pi\)
⭐ KEY TRIGONOMETRIC INTEGRALS (needed in polar integration) \[\int_0^{2\pi}\sin\theta\,d\theta=0,\quad\int_0^\pi\sin\theta\,d\theta=2,\quad\int_0^\pi\cos\theta\,d\theta=0\] \[\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^2\theta\,d\theta=\pi,\quad\int_0^\pi\sin^2\theta\,d\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}\] \[\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^2\theta\,d\theta=\pi,\quad\int_0^{2\pi}\cos\theta\sin\theta\,d\theta=0\]

4.3 PYQs — Polar Double Integrals 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PYQs — POLAR COORDINATES

Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x^2}}(x^2+y^2)\,dy\,dx\) by changing to polar coordinates.
Winter 2023 7 Marks 🔥 Classic polar conversion
💡 The region is the first-quadrant part of the unit circle (\(0\leq x\leq 1\), \(0\leq y\leq\sqrt{1-x^2}\)). Integrand is \(x^2+y^2=r^2\). Perfect for polar.
POLAR LIMITS: First-quadrant unit circle: \(0\leq r\leq 1\), \(0\leq\theta\leq\pi/2\).
TRANSFORM: \[x^2+y^2 = r^2,\quad dA = r\,dr\,d\theta\] \[\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^1 r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^1 r^3\,dr\,d\theta\]
INNER: \[\int_0^1 r^3\,dr = \left[\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{4}\]
OUTER: \[\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{1}{4}\,d\theta = \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi}{8}\]
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{\pi}{8}\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^{\sqrt{2x-x^2}}\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}\,dy\,dx\) by changing to polar coordinates.
Summer 2024 7 Marks
💡 \(y=\sqrt{2x-x^2}\Rightarrow y^2=2x-x^2\Rightarrow x^2-2x+y^2=0\Rightarrow(x-1)^2+y^2=1\). This is a circle of radius 1 centred at \((1,0)\). In polar: \(r=2\cos\theta\), \(0\leq\theta\leq\pi/2\).
POLAR REGION: Circle \((x-1)^2+y^2=1\): in polar, \(r^2-2r\cos\theta=0\Rightarrow r=2\cos\theta\). So \(0\leq r\leq 2\cos\theta\), \(0\leq\theta\leq\pi/2\).
TRANSFORM: \(x=r\cos\theta\), \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=r\), \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\): \[\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{2\cos\theta}\frac{r\cos\theta}{r}\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{2\cos\theta}r\cos\theta\,dr\,d\theta\]
INNER: \[\cos\theta\int_0^{2\cos\theta}r\,dr = \cos\theta\cdot\frac{4\cos^2\theta}{2} = 2\cos^3\theta\]
OUTER: \[\int_0^{\pi/2}2\cos^3\theta\,d\theta = 2\cdot\frac{2}{3} = \frac{4}{3}\] (Using Wallis: \(\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^3\theta\,d\theta=\frac{2}{3}\))
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{4}{3}\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\iint_R r\sqrt{a^2-r^2}\,dr\,d\theta\) over the upper half of the circle \(r=a\cos\theta\).
Winter 2025 4 Marks
LIMITS: Upper half of \(r=a\cos\theta\): \(0\leq r\leq a\cos\theta\), \(0\leq\theta\leq\pi/2\).
INNER (w.r.t. \(r\)): \[\int_0^{a\cos\theta}r\sqrt{a^2-r^2}\,dr\] Let \(u=a^2-r^2\Rightarrow du=-2r\,dr\): at \(r=0\), \(u=a^2\); at \(r=a\cos\theta\), \(u=a^2\sin^2\theta\). \[=-\frac{1}{2}\int_{a^2}^{a^2\sin^2\theta}\sqrt{u}\,du = \frac{1}{2}\int_{a^2\sin^2\theta}^{a^2}u^{1/2}\,du = \frac{1}{3}\Big[u^{3/2}\Big]_{a^2\sin^2\theta}^{a^2} = \frac{a^3}{3}(1-\sin^3\theta)\]
OUTER: \[\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{a^3}{3}(1-\sin^3\theta)\,d\theta = \frac{a^3}{3}\left[\frac{\pi}{2}-\frac{2}{3}\right] = \frac{a^3}{3}\cdot\frac{3\pi-4}{6} = \frac{a^3(3\pi-4)}{18}\]
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{a^3(3\pi-4)}{18}\)
TOPIC 5

Applications — Area, Volume, and Centre of Mass

5.1 Area Using Double Integrals ⭐ High Priority

⭐ AREA FORMULAS \[\text{Area of }R = \iint_R dA = \iint_R 1\,dA\]

In Cartesian: set \(f=1\) and integrate.

In Polar: \(\text{Area} = \int\int r\,dr\,d\theta\)

5.2 Volume Under a Surface 🔥 Most Important

⭐ VOLUME FORMULAS

Volume under \(z=f(x,y)\) above region \(R\): \[V = \iint_R f(x,y)\,dA\]

Volume between two surfaces \(z_1\leq z\leq z_2\): \[V = \iint_R [z_2(x,y)-z_1(x,y)]\,dA\]

Volume using polar: \[V = \int\int f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta\]

5.3 Centre of Mass and Gravity ⭐ High Priority

⭐ CENTRE OF MASS FORMULAS

For a lamina with density \(\delta(x,y)\):

QuantityFormula
Mass \(M\)\(\displaystyle M = \iint_R\delta(x,y)\,dA\)
First moment about \(y\)-axis\(\displaystyle M_y = \iint_R x\cdot\delta(x,y)\,dA\)
First moment about \(x\)-axis\(\displaystyle M_x = \iint_R y\cdot\delta(x,y)\,dA\)
Centre of mass \((\bar{x},\bar{y})\)\(\bar{x}=M_y/M,\quad\bar{y}=M_x/M\)

5.4 PYQs — Applications 🔥 Most Important

🎯 PYQs — AREA, VOLUME, CENTRE OF MASS

Q: Find the area of region \(R\) bounded by \(y=2x^2\) and \(y^2=4x\).
Summer 2025 4 Marks
INTERSECTION: \(y=2x^2\) and \(y^2=4x\Rightarrow(2x^2)^2=4x\Rightarrow 4x^4=4x\Rightarrow x=0,1\). Points: \((0,0)\) and \((1,2)\).
REGION for \(0\leq x\leq 1\): from \(y=2x^2\) (bottom) to \(y=2\sqrt{x}\) (top).
AREA: \[\int_0^1(2\sqrt{x}-2x^2)\,dx=\left[\frac{4x^{3/2}}{3}-\frac{2x^3}{3}\right]_0^1=\frac{4}{3}-\frac{2}{3}=\frac{2}{3}\]
∴ Area \(= \dfrac{2}{3}\) square units.
Q: Find volume of solid that lies under paraboloid \(z=x^2+y^2\) above region bounded by \(y=2x\) and \(y=x^2\).
Summer 2025 7 Marks 🔥 Important application
REGION: \(y=2x\) and \(y=x^2\): intersection at \(x=0,2\). For \(0\leq x\leq 2\): \(x^2\leq y\leq 2x\).
VOLUME: \[V=\int_0^2\int_{x^2}^{2x}(x^2+y^2)\,dy\,dx\]
INNER: \[\int_{x^2}^{2x}(x^2+y^2)\,dy = \left[x^2y+\frac{y^3}{3}\right]_{x^2}^{2x} = \left(2x^3+\frac{8x^3}{3}\right)-\left(x^4+\frac{x^6}{3}\right) = \frac{14x^3}{3}-x^4-\frac{x^6}{3}\]
OUTER: \[\int_0^2\left(\frac{14x^3}{3}-x^4-\frac{x^6}{3}\right)dx = \left[\frac{14x^4}{12}-\frac{x^5}{5}-\frac{x^7}{21}\right]_0^2 = \frac{224}{12}-\frac{32}{5}-\frac{128}{21}\] \[= \frac{56}{3}-\frac{32}{5}-\frac{128}{21} = \frac{1960-672-384}{105} \cdot\frac{105}{105}= \frac{216}{35}\]
∴ Volume \(= \dfrac{216}{35}\)
Q: A thin plate covers the triangular region bounded by \(x\)-axis, \(x=1\) and \(y=2x\). Density \(\delta(x,y)=6x+6y+6\). Find plate's mass, first moments and centre of mass.
Winter 2024 7 Marks 🔥 Centre of Mass
💡 Region: \(0\leq x\leq 1\), \(0\leq y\leq 2x\).
MASS \(M\): \[M=\int_0^1\int_0^{2x}(6x+6y+6)\,dy\,dx=\int_0^1\left[6xy+3y^2+6y\right]_0^{2x}dx\] \[=\int_0^1(12x^2+12x^2+12x)\,dx=\int_0^1(24x^2+12x)\,dx=\left[8x^3+6x^2\right]_0^1=14\]
MOMENT \(M_y=\iint x\delta\,dA\): \[=\int_0^1\int_0^{2x}x(6x+6y+6)\,dy\,dx=\int_0^1 x\left[6xy+3y^2+6y\right]_0^{2x}dx\] \[=\int_0^1 x(24x^2+12x)\,dx=\int_0^1(24x^3+12x^2)\,dx=6+4=10\]
MOMENT \(M_x=\iint y\delta\,dA\): \[=\int_0^1\int_0^{2x}y(6x+6y+6)\,dy\,dx=\int_0^1\left[3xy^2+2y^3+3y^2\right]_0^{2x}dx\] \[=\int_0^1(12x^3+16x^3+12x^2)\,dx=\int_0^1(28x^3+12x^2)\,dx=7+4=11\]
CENTRE OF MASS: \[\bar{x}=\frac{M_y}{M}=\frac{10}{14}=\frac{5}{7},\quad\bar{y}=\frac{M_x}{M}=\frac{11}{14}\]
Mass \(=14\), \(M_y=10\), \(M_x=11\). Centre of mass: \(\left(\dfrac{5}{7},\dfrac{11}{14}\right)\).
Q: Find volume of solid bounded by paraboloid \(z=1-x^2-y^2\), plane \(z=1\) and XY-plane.
Winter 2025 7 Marks
💡 Paraboloid \(z=1-r^2\) meets XY-plane at \(z=0\Rightarrow r=1\). Use polar coordinates.
REGION: \(x^2+y^2\leq 1\) (where paraboloid is above XY-plane). Polar: \(0\leq r\leq 1\), \(0\leq\theta\leq 2\pi\).
VOLUME (height \(= z = 1-r^2\)): \[V=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1(1-r^2)\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=2\pi\int_0^1(r-r^3)\,dr=2\pi\left[\frac{r^2}{2}-\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1=2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{4}=\frac{\pi}{2}\]
∴ Volume \(= \dfrac{\pi}{2}\)
Q: Sketch region bounded by \(x=0,x=2,y=x,y=x+2\). Find area by double integrals.
Winter 2022 7 Marks
REGION: \(0\leq x\leq 2\), \(x\leq y\leq x+2\).
AREA: \[A=\int_0^2\int_x^{x+2}dy\,dx=\int_0^2 2\,dx=4\]
∴ Area \(=4\) square units.
TOPIC 6

Triple Integrals

6.1 Triple Integrals — Setup and Evaluation 🔥 Most Important

📖 TRIPLE INTEGRAL \[\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV = \int_a^b\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)}\int_{h_1(x,y)}^{h_2(x,y)} f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx\]

Evaluate from innermost to outermost: first integrate w.r.t. \(z\) (treating \(x,y\) as constants), then \(y\), then \(x\).

🔧 METHOD FOR RECTANGULAR BOX

For \(B=[a,b]\times[c,d]\times[e,f]\), all limits are constants: \[\iiint_B f\,dV = \int_a^b\int_c^d\int_e^f f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx\]

Evaluate three single integrals from inside out.

6.2 Cylindrical and Spherical Coordinates ⭐ High Priority

⭐ CYLINDRICAL COORDINATES
VariableRelation
\(x = r\cos\theta\)\(r^2=x^2+y^2\)
\(y = r\sin\theta\)\(\tan\theta=y/x\)
\(z = z\)\(z=z\)
\(dV\)\(= r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz\)

Use when: region has circular cross-sections (cylinders, cones).

⭐ SPHERICAL COORDINATES
VariableRelation
\(x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\)\(\rho\) = distance from origin
\(y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\)\(\phi\) = angle from \(z\)-axis
\(z = \rho\cos\phi\)\(\theta\) = angle in \(xy\)-plane
\(dV\)\(=\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta\)

Use when: region involves spheres, \(\rho^2=x^2+y^2+z^2\).

6.3 PYQs — Triple Integrals 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PYQs — TRIPLE INTEGRALS

Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_x^1\int_0^{y-x}dz\,dy\,dx\)
Winter 2022 / Winter 2024 3 Marks 🔥 Repeated
INNER (w.r.t. \(z\), 0 to \(y-x\)): \[\int_0^{y-x}dz = y-x\]
MIDDLE (w.r.t. \(y\), \(x\) to 1): \[\int_x^1(y-x)\,dy = \left[\frac{y^2}{2}-xy\right]_x^1 = \left(\frac{1}{2}-x\right)-\left(\frac{x^2}{2}-x^2\right) = \frac{1}{2}-x+\frac{x^2}{2}\]
OUTER (w.r.t. \(x\), 0 to 1): \[\int_0^1\left(\frac{1}{2}-x+\frac{x^2}{2}\right)dx = \left[\frac{x}{2}-\frac{x^2}{2}+\frac{x^3}{6}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{6}\]
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{1}{6}\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\iiint_B xyz^2\,dV\) where \(B=\{(x,y,z): 0\leq x\leq 1,\;-1\leq y\leq 2,\;0\leq z\leq 3\}\).
Summer 2025 3 Marks
💡 Rectangular box — all limits constant, so the triple integral separates into a product of three single integrals!
SEPARATE (since \(f=xyz^2 = x\cdot y\cdot z^2\)): \[\iiint_B xyz^2\,dV = \left(\int_0^1 x\,dx\right)\left(\int_{-1}^2 y\,dy\right)\left(\int_0^3 z^2\,dz\right)\]
EVALUATE EACH: \[\int_0^1 x\,dx=\frac{1}{2},\quad\int_{-1}^2 y\,dy=\left[\frac{y^2}{2}\right]_{-1}^2=2-\frac{1}{2}=\frac{3}{2},\quad\int_0^3 z^2\,dz=9\]
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{1}{2}\times\dfrac{3}{2}\times 9 = \dfrac{27}{4}\)
For product-form integrands over rectangular boxes: split into product of 1D integrals — saves a lot of time!
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_1^2\int_0^{yz} xyz\,dx\,dy\,dz\)
Summer 2025 (OR) 3 Marks
INNER (w.r.t. \(x\), 0 to \(yz\)): \[\int_0^{yz}xyz\,dx = yz\cdot\frac{x^2}{2}\bigg]_0^{yz} = yz\cdot\frac{y^2z^2}{2} = \frac{y^3z^3}{2}\]
MIDDLE (w.r.t. \(y\), 1 to 2): \[\int_1^2\frac{y^3z^3}{2}\,dy = \frac{z^3}{2}\cdot\frac{y^4}{4}\bigg]_1^2 = \frac{z^3}{2}\cdot\frac{15}{4} = \frac{15z^3}{8}\]
OUTER (w.r.t. \(z\), 0 to 2): \[\int_0^2\frac{15z^3}{8}\,dz = \frac{15}{8}\cdot\frac{z^4}{4}\bigg]_0^2 = \frac{15}{8}\cdot 4 = \frac{15}{2}\]
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{15}{2}\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^1\int_{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}^2 z\,dz\,dy\,dx\)
Winter 2025 4 Marks
INNER (w.r.t. \(z\)): \[\int_{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}^2 z\,dz = \left[\frac{z^2}{2}\right]_{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}^2 = 2-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2}\]
REMAINING DOUBLE INTEGRAL: \[\int_0^1\int_0^1\left(2-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2}\right)dy\,dx\] Inner (w.r.t. \(y\)): \(\int_0^1\left(2-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2}\right)dy = \left[2y-\frac{x^2y}{2}-\frac{y^3}{6}\right]_0^1 = 2-\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{1}{6}\) Outer (w.r.t. \(x\)): \(\int_0^1\left(\frac{11}{6}-\frac{x^2}{2}\right)dx = \frac{11}{6}-\frac{1}{6}=\frac{10}{6}=\frac{5}{3}\)
∴ Answer \(= \dfrac{5}{3}\)
Q: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^{2x}\int_0^{2xy}2xyz\,dz\,dy\,dx\)
Summer 2023 3 Marks
INNER (w.r.t. \(z\), 0 to \(2xy\)): \[\int_0^{2xy}2xyz\,dz = 2xy\cdot\frac{z^2}{2}\bigg]_0^{2xy} = 2xy\cdot 2x^2y^2 = 4x^3y^3\]
MIDDLE (w.r.t. \(y\), 0 to \(2x\)): \[\int_0^{2x}4x^3y^3\,dy = 4x^3\cdot\frac{y^4}{4}\bigg]_0^{2x} = x^3\cdot 16x^4 = 16x^7\]
OUTER (w.r.t. \(x\), 0 to 2): \[\int_0^2 16x^7\,dx = 16\cdot\frac{x^8}{8}\bigg]_0^2 = 2\cdot 256 = 512\]
∴ Answer \(= 512\)
