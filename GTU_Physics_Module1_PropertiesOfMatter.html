TOPIC 1

Elasticity and Stress-Strain Relationship

1.1 Stress and Strain — Definitions 🔥 Most Important

📖 STRESS

When an external force is applied to a body, internal restoring forces develop. Stress is the internal restoring force per unit area:

\[\text{Stress } (\sigma) = \frac{\text{Applied Force } (F)}{\text{Area } (A)}\]

SI Unit: N/m² or Pascal (Pa)

📖 STRAIN

Strain is the ratio of change in dimension to original dimension. It has no unit (dimensionless quantity).

TypeFormula
Longitudinal Strain\(\displaystyle\frac{\Delta L}{L}\)
Volumetric Strain\(\displaystyle\frac{\Delta V}{V}\)
Shear Strain\(\displaystyle\theta = \frac{x}{L}\) (angle of deformation)
💡 INTUITION Think of stress as "how hard you push" and strain as "how much it squishes". A stiff material (steel) has low strain for high stress. A rubber band has high strain for low stress.

1.2 Hooke's Law and Modulus of Elasticity 🔥 Most Important

⭐ HOOKE'S LAW

Within elastic limit, stress is directly proportional to strain:

\[\sigma \propto \epsilon \quad \Rightarrow \quad \sigma = E\epsilon\]

where \(E\) = Modulus of Elasticity (Young's Modulus for longitudinal stress)

📖 MODULUS OF ELASTICITY

The ratio of stress to strain is constant for a given material (within elastic limit). This constant is called Modulus of Elasticity.

TypeFormulaSymbol
Young's Modulus\(\displaystyle Y = \frac{\text{Longitudinal Stress}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}} = \frac{F/A}{\Delta L/L}\)Y or E
Shear Modulus\(\displaystyle G = \frac{\text{Shear Stress}}{\text{Shear Strain}} = \frac{F/A}{\theta}\)G or η
Bulk Modulus\(\displaystyle K = \frac{\text{Volume Stress}}{\text{Volume Strain}} = \frac{P}{\Delta V/V}\)K or B

1.3 Poisson's Ratio ⭐ High Priority

📖 POISSON'S RATIO (σ)

When a wire is stretched, it becomes longer but thinner. The ratio of lateral strain to longitudinal strain is called Poisson's ratio:

\[\sigma = \frac{\text{Lateral Strain}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}} = \frac{-\Delta D/D}{\Delta L/L}\]

Theoretical limits: -1 to 0.5
Practical limits: 0 to 0.5
For most metals: 0.25 to 0.35

⭐ RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN ELASTIC CONSTANTS
\(Y = 2G(1+\sigma)\)(Young's vs Shear)
\(Y = 3K(1-2\sigma)\)(Young's vs Bulk)
\(\displaystyle \sigma = \frac{3K-2G}{6K+2G}\)(Poisson's ratio)

1.4 PYQs — Elasticity 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PREVIOUS YEAR QUESTIONS — ELASTICITY

Q: Define Poisson's ratio and state its limits.
Winter 2024 / Summer 2023 3 Marks 🔥 Repeated
💡 Definition question — write clearly with formula and limits.
DEFINITION: Poisson's ratio is the ratio of lateral strain to longitudinal strain when a material is stretched within its elastic limit.
FORMULA: \(\sigma = \frac{\text{Lateral Strain}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}} = \frac{-\Delta D/D}{\Delta L/L}\)
LIMITS: Theoretical: -1 to +0.5; Practical: 0 to 0.5; For metals: 0.25 to 0.35
Write all three points for full marks (3 marks).
Q: Derive the relationship between Young's modulus, Shear modulus and Poisson's ratio.
Summer 2025 4 Marks 🔥 Important
💡 Standard derivation. Consider a cube under shear stress and analyze diagonal strain.
STEP 1: Consider a cube ABCD subjected to shear stress τ on opposite faces.
STEP 2: Shear strain θ = τ/G (by definition of shear modulus)
STEP 3: Diagonal AC elongates due to tensile stress on diagonal. Longitudinal strain in diagonal = τ(1+σ)/Y
STEP 4: Geometrically, diagonal strain = θ/2 = τ/(2G)
STEP 5: Equating: τ/(2G) = τ(1+σ)/Y
∴ Y = 2G(1+σ) ✓ Proved
TOPIC 2

Beam Bending and Deflection

2.1 Bending Moment and Radius of Curvature 🔥 Most Important

📖 BENDING MOMENT

When a beam is loaded, internal moments develop to resist bending. The Bending Moment at any section is the algebraic sum of moments of all forces on either side of that section.

⭐ FLEXURE FORMULA (BENDING EQUATION) \[\frac{M}{I} = \frac{E}{R} = \frac{\sigma}{y}\]

where:
M = Bending Moment
I = Moment of Inertia of cross-section
E = Young's Modulus
R = Radius of curvature of bent beam
σ = Bending stress at distance y from neutral axis

💡 INTUITION When you bend a ruler, the top surface stretches (tension) and bottom compresses. The middle layer (neutral axis) remains unchanged. Bending moment quantifies how much "bending effort" is applied.

2.2 Deflection of Cantilever ⭐ High Priority

📖 CANTILEVER BEAM

A beam fixed at one end and free at the other is called a cantilever. When loaded at the free end, it deflects (bends downward).

⭐ DEPRESSION (DEFLECTION) AT FREE END

For a cantilever of length L, loaded with force W at free end:

\[\delta = \frac{WL^3}{3EI}\]

For rectangular cross-section: \(I = \frac{bd^3}{12}\)
For circular cross-section: \(I = \frac{\pi r^4}{4}\)

🔧 KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER
  • Deflection δ ∝ L³ (cube of length) — doubling length increases deflection 8x!
  • δ ∝ 1/E — stiffer material (higher E) means less deflection
  • δ ∝ 1/I — thicker beam (higher I) means less deflection

2.3 PYQs — Beam Bending 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PREVIOUS YEAR QUESTIONS — BEAM BENDING

Q: Derive the expression for depression at the free end of a cantilever beam loaded at the free end.
Winter 2025 / Summer 2024 7 Marks 🔥 Most Important
💡 Standard derivation using bending moment and differential equation of elastic curve.
STEP 1: Consider a cantilever of length L fixed at A and loaded with W at free end B.
STEP 2: At distance x from fixed end, bending moment M = -W(L-x)
STEP 3: From flexure formula: \(M = EI\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\)
STEP 4: So: \(EI\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -W(L-x)\)
STEP 5: Integrate once: \(EI\frac{dy}{dx} = -WLx + \frac{Wx^2}{2} + C_1\)
STEP 6: At x=0, dy/dx=0 (fixed end), so C₁=0
STEP 7: Integrate again: \(EIy = -\frac{WLx^2}{2} + \frac{Wx^3}{6} + C_2\)
STEP 8: At x=0, y=0 (fixed end), so C₂=0
STEP 9: At free end x=L: \(y = \delta = \frac{1}{EI}\left(-\frac{WL^3}{2} + \frac{WL^3}{6}\right) = -\frac{WL^3}{3EI}\)
∴ Depression at free end: \(\delta = \frac{WL^3}{3EI}\) (negative sign indicates downward deflection)
TOPIC 3

Fluid Mechanics and Viscosity

3.1 Bernoulli's Equation ⭐ High Priority

⭐ BERNOULLI'S EQUATION

For steady, incompressible, non-viscous flow:

\[P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gh = \text{constant}\]

where:
P = Pressure energy per unit volume
½ρv² = Kinetic energy per unit volume
ρgh = Potential energy per unit volume

💡 INTUITION Bernoulli's principle says: faster fluid = lower pressure. This is why airplane wings generate lift (air moves faster over curved top) and why roofs blow off in storms (fast wind above = low pressure).

3.2 Coefficient of Viscosity 🔥 Most Important

📖 VISCOSITY

Viscosity is the property of a fluid that opposes the relative motion between its layers. It is "internal friction" of the fluid.

⭐ NEWTON'S LAW OF VISCOSITY

The viscous drag F between two layers is:

\[F = \eta A \frac{dv}{dx}\]

where η = coefficient of viscosity, A = area, dv/dx = velocity gradient

SI Unit: Pa·s or N·s/m²
CGS Unit: poise (1 Pa·s = 10 poise)

⭐ POISEUILLE'S EQUATION

For laminar flow through a capillary tube of radius r and length L:

\[Q = \frac{\pi P r^4}{8\eta L}\]

where Q = volume flow rate, P = pressure difference

Note: Flow rate ∝ r⁴ — doubling radius increases flow 16x!

3.3 PYQs — Fluid Mechanics 🔥 Most Important

🎯 ALL PREVIOUS YEAR QUESTIONS — FLUID MECHANICS

Q: Define coefficient of viscosity. State its SI unit and dimensional formula.
Summer 2025 / Winter 2023 3 Marks 🔥 Repeated
💡 Direct definition question.
DEFINITION: Coefficient of viscosity is the ratio of shear stress to velocity gradient in a fluid.
FORMULA: \(\eta = \frac{F}{A(dv/dx)}\)
SI UNIT: Pa·s or N·s/m²
DIMENSIONS: [M¹L⁻¹T⁻¹]
Write all four points for full marks.
Q: State and prove Bernoulli's theorem for fluid flow.
Winter 2024 4 Marks ⭐ Important
💡 Use work-energy theorem for proof.
STATEMENT: For steady flow of incompressible, non-viscous fluid, total energy per unit volume remains constant.
PROOF: Consider fluid element moving from point 1 to 2. Work done by pressure = change in KE + PE.
Work by pressure: W = (P₁-P₂)ΔV
Change in KE: ΔKE = ½m(v₂²-v₁²) = ½ρΔV(v₂²-v₁²)
Change in PE: ΔPE = mg(h₂-h₁) = ρΔVg(h₂-h₁)
Equating: (P₁-P₂) = ½ρ(v₂²-v₁²) + ρg(h₂-h₁)
∴ P₁ + ½ρv₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + ½ρv₂² + ρgh₂ = constant ✓
